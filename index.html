The premise

The model was never the hard part. The hard part is figuring out what one person actually does all day — and building something that does it for them, and keeps doing it when nobody's watching. I came to this sideways, through linguistics — the study of how meaning gets built and how it quietly falls apart. Turns out that's the same problem as making an AI system behave: precise language in, predictable behavior out. Which is why I don't think the scarce skill is writing code. Plenty of people write code. The scarce skill is taste — knowing what to build, what to keep boring and deterministic, and what to leave alone entirely. Most of my best decisions have been things I talked someone out of.

How I think about it

Principles I build by

→ Specificity beats generality

General-purpose AI rollouts look impressive and quietly fail. Systems built around one real workflow get used. I'd rather solve one person's actual task completely than gesture at solving everyone's.

→ The boring parts are the product

Deduplication, validation, error handling, voice enforcement. Nobody demos these. They're the entire reason a system survives contact with reality.

→ Deterministic until proven otherwise

Agentic architecture is a tool, not a default. Most problems want a reliable pipeline. Knowing when not to reach for autonomy is a senior signal.

→ Language is the interface

The quality of what comes out of an AI system is bounded by the precision of what goes in. That's a linguistics problem before it's an engineering one.

Free-text → live campaign engine

From a sentence to a deduplicated outbound sequence in about a minute

~20-node n8n pipeline
Vercel frontend
Claude via OpenRouter
Apollo enrichment
CRM dedup via cloud function

You type a plain-English description of who you want to reach. The system parses it into structured targeting filters, pulls and enriches matching prospects, writes a multi-step email sequence in a defined brand voice, checks its own output for hallucinations, dedupes against the CRM so you never accidentally cold-email someone you're already mid-deal with, and pushes the whole thing live. Sentence in, campaign out, hands off.

The part I'm proud of isn't that it's fast — it's that it's not an agent. The LLM does the language; the orchestration does the thinking. Autonomy would've been more fun to demo and far worse to rely on. Choosing boring-and-reliable on purpose is the whole job.
